Immunoengineering for cardiovascular disease

Noor Momin

The Stephenson Foundation Term Assistant Professor of Innovation at Penn Bioengineering. Her Immunoengineering for Health lab combines cardiovascular immunology, protein engineering and pharmacokinetic modeling to develop targeted therapies for cardiovascular disease, with extensions into autoimmunity and cancer. The lab designs antibody-decorated nanoparticles and engineered cytokines that direct immunomodulatory payloads to inflamed tissues — including arterial plaques and post-infarct myocardium — to dampen pathological inflammation and promote healing without the systemic toxicity that has limited prior immunotherapies. Recent work spans matrix-targeted IL-4 for cardiac healing after MI, B-cell-driven autoantibody contributions to atrial fibrillation (Nature Cardiovascular Research, 2025), and a resistin-like molecule gamma collaboration on ventricular tachycardia (Science, 2025). PhD, MIT Biological Engineering. A patented cytokine immunotherapy from her doctoral work has been licensed to Cullinan Oncology.

Univ. of Pennsylvania — Bioengineering

Generative biologic design

Pranam Chatterjee

Assistant Professor of Bioengineering and Computer & Information Science at Penn. His Programmable Biology Group develops new generative models for the de novo design of peptide and protein biologics, spanning algorithmic theory through pre-clinical validation in vitro and in vivo. The lab develops therapeutic interventions for rare neurodegenerative diseases, pediatric sarcomas, viral infections, infertility and substance use disorders. SB, SM and PhD from MIT. Recipient of the NIH MIRA Award and the Hartwell Individual Biomedical Research Award. Co-founder of Gameto, UbiquiTx and AtomBioworks.

Penn Bioengineering & CIS

Neural pathways of food intake & body weight

Matthew Hayes

Albert J. Stunkard Professor in Psychiatry at the Perelman School of Medicine. Vice Chair of Basic and Translational Neuroscience and director of the Molecular and Neural Basis of Psychiatric Disease Section. DNA-loaded lipid nanoparticles

Jake Brenner

Assistant professor and physician in Penn's Department of Medicine. Cares for ICU patients and runs a genetic medicine lab. His lab developed the first safe and effective DNA-loaded lipid nanoparticles (DNA-LNPs), enabling expression of any size protein for >1 year per dose, in just the chosen cell type. To commercialize the DNA-LNPs the lab spun out ClearXpression, aiming to bring gene therapy to common chronic diseases ranging from osteoarthritis to atherosclerosis. In 2025 the DNA-LNPs received the CRS Gene Therapy Scientific Achievement award and Falling Walls listed them as a Top 10 Breakthrough of the Year.

Penn Medicine / ClearXpression

Inhaled AAV gene therapy for cystic fibrosis

Roland Kolbeck

Spirovant's transformative technology addresses potency challenges in gene therapy by administering AAV with Augmenter directly to the lungs. The clinical-stage lead program, SP-101, is the first and only inhaled AAV developed with an inhaled Augmenter (doxorubicin) that enhances transgene expression by 100–1,000×, targeting the unmet needs of CF patients who do not benefit from small molecule modulators.

Mark Yarmarkovich

Discovery and immunotherapeutic targeting of intracellular cancer drivers using peptide-centric chimeric antigen receptors (PC-CARs). Demonstrated potent killing of neuroblastoma cells and complete tumor regression in mice.
